Anxiety comes from two areas of the brain: the cortex and the amygdala.

The **cortex** is responsible for reasoning, language, mathematics, and more. In addition to providing sights, sounds, and other perceptions, the cortex also attaches meaning and memories to them. This is the reason why I see a friend and not just a person. The cortex continuously evaluates environmental stimuli for potential threats. Overactive threat detecting can lead to perceiving neutral situations as dangerous which can lead to anxiety. We humans also have the ability to anticipate and create worry. We're able to predict future events and imagine their consequences which can also trigger anxieties. 

The **amygdala** attaches emotional significance to objects or situations to form *emotional memories* that we are not consciously aware of. Especially those that trigger anxiety. These anxieties feel like they have no apparent cause or logical sense. For example, a Vietnam veteran changes their soap to a different brand and suddenly starts experiencing anxiety. They later discover that this was the same soap they used during their deployment. The mere smell of it brought them back and triggered their anxiety without their knowledge. 

Put simply, the amygdala-based anxieties are created by associations and cortex-based anxieties are created by thoughts and interpretations.

The thalamus sends sensory information to both the cortex and the amygdala, however, the amygdala processes the information before the cortex does. This means the amygdala reacts quickly to protect us from danger before the cortex can even parse what the danger is. This is the fight or flight response at work. The amygdala is not only faster, it can also override other brain processes. It's literally true you can't think when it has taken control.

## I Have Anticipation / Worry Anxiety

- When I think something might go wrong, it's always on my mind
- I can be worried sick months before something occurs
- I consider situations if there's potential for danger or illness
- I waste time thinking about solutions to nonexistent problems

## Controlling My Anxieties

These are steps to help lower my anxiety. The order was given by the book:

### 1. Use Relaxation, Sleep, and Exercise to Reduce Sympathetic Nervous System Activation

- Slow, deep breathing — 4-7-8 breathing exercise
- Diaphragmatic breathing — Breathing through the stomach
- Progressive muscle relaxation
- Using imagery or visualization
- Meditating

### 2. Monitor My Thinking for Any Anxiety-igniting Thoughts. Then Replace Anxiety-igniting Thoughts with Coping Thoughts

- Cognitive defusion — Be an observer of my cortex, not a believer
- Thought stopping — Immediately stop anxiety creating thoughts and replace them with positive thoughts
- Planning — If I anticipate a situation will actually arise, come up with possible solutions then move on from the thought

### 3. Design Exposure Exercises that Can Modify My Amygdala's Response to Triggers

- Experience sights, sounds, and other stimuli that create anxiety in order to activate neural circuitry that holds these emotional memories
- It is imperative that when I start exposure therapy, I cannot back out until the anxieties subside. Quitting only teaches my amygdala that I will feel better if I do
